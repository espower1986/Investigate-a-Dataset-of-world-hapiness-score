Introduction¶

In this project we use The data is obtained from Gapminder World https://www.gapminder.org/data/ to analyze happiness score around the world as dependent variable, This is the national average response to the question of life evaluations asking the following “Please imagine a ladder, with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?” This measure is also referred to as Cantril life ladder
In order to find out influences on this score, 4 independents variables are used:
-Income: Gross domestic product per person adjusted for differences in purchasing power (in international dollars, fixed 2011 prices, PPP based on 2011 ICP).

-Democracy index:This index is using the data from the Economist Inteligence Unit to express the quality of democracies as a number between 0 and 100. It's based on 60 different aspects of societies that are relevant to democracy universal suffrage for all adults, voter participation, perception of human rights protection and freedom to form organizations and parties. The democracy index is calculated from the 60 indicators, divided into five "sub indexes", which are:

  1. Electoral pluralism index;
  2. Government index;
  3. Political participation indexm;
  4. Political culture index;
  5. Civil liberty index. The sub-indexes are based on the sum of scores on roughly 12 indicators per sub-index, converted into a score between 0 and 100. (The Economist publishes the index with a scale from 0 to 10, but Gapminder has converted it to 0 to 100 to make it easier to communicate as a percentage.)

-Life expactancy: The average number of years a newborn child would live if current mortality patterns were to stay the same.

-Human Development Index: is an index used to rank countries by level of "human development". It contains three dimensions: health level, educational level and living standard.

-in order to group by continent a data set containing countries with their continent will be used

Research Question¶

  • Research question 1: Are money makes happiness ? (influence of income on hapiscore)
  • Research question 2: Are the democratic systems make their people happier in hight income countries ?
  • Research question 3: In low income countries do governments' efforts in human development make their people happier?
  • Research question 4: what is the caractiques of the happiest countries?
  • Research question 5: what are most important variable in predicting the hapiness score ?
  • Research question 6: What is the evolution over time of the hapiscore on the worldwide scale and for each continent ?
  • Research question 7: Are the people in happiest countrys live's longer ? (influence of hapiscore on life expectancy)

Data Cleaning¶

To fill in missing data in hapiscore and Human Development Index dataframes, we will apply the following rules:

1) in case the pourcentage of missing data for each row is over than a limit (50%) we drop the row from the data frame
2) in case the serie of missing data is preceded and followed by real measurements: we will assume that the evolution is progressive over time between the previous and next real measurement.
3) in case the serie of missing data is not preceded by real measurement we will use the previous real measurement to fill in this serie
4) in case the serie of missing data is not followed by real measurement we will use the next real measurement to fill in this serie

To apply this approach we must create 3 functions to be able to reuse it if necessary:
1) missing_location: this function will target the missing data location in a list, This function will return a list of tuples each tuple indicate the 4 informations about NaN serie in the list (starting index of the NaN serie, ending index of NaN serie, previous real measurement, next real measurement )
2) fill_missing_list: this function will use missing_location result and it will replace the missing data according to previous (2,3 and 4) rules finaly it will return a full list
3) fill_missing_df: this Function will ask for dataframe, start row, end row, start column, end column and the limit pourcentage as inputs, and it will drop the row if the pourcetage of missing values exceed the limit (50%) (1st rule), if not it will use the two previous functions to fill in the missing data in the dataframe

#Creation of a function to target the location of missing data in a list 
#This function will return a list of tuples each tuple indicate the 4 informations about NaN serie in the list
# 4 informations: (starting index of the NaN serie, ending index of NaN serie, previous real measurement, next real measurement )
def missing_location(my_list):
    
    tpl=() # temporary tuple
    tuple_list=[] #list of tuples
    start_na=0 #starting index of the NaN serie
    end_na=0 #ending index on NaN serie

    up_value=math.nan #previous real measurement 
    down_value=math.nan #next real measurement
    left=True #Boolean for precision if we are looking for (start index or end index)
    
#Creation of list of tuples:
    #exception if the first list value is NaN
    if np.isnan(my_list[0]):
        start_na=0
        up_value=math.nan
        left=False
    #loop in the list to create the tuples
    for i in range(len(my_list)):
        #in case we found a beginning of the NaN serie
        if np.isnan(my_list[i]) and left:
            start_na=i
            up_value=my_list[i-1]
            left=False
        #in case we found a real value and we have already found start index
        elif not np.isnan(my_list[i]) and not left:
            end_na=i-1
            down_value=my_list[i]
            tpl=(start_na,end_na,up_value,down_value)
            left=True
            tuple_list.append(tpl)
        #in cas we get the last element in the list and we already found the start index
        if i==len(my_list)-1 and np.isnan(my_list[i]) and not left:
            tpl=(start_na,i,up_value,math.nan)
            tuple_list.append(tpl)

        #in cas where we get the last element in the list and we did not found the start index
        elif i==len(my_list)-1 and np.isnan(my_list[i]) and left:
            tpl=(i,i,math.nan,math.nan)
            tuple_list.append(tpl)
    return tuple_list

# Create function to replace missing data in a list
# by giving the missing data locations(tuple_list) as input
def fill_missing_list(old_list, tuple_list):
    #copy the original list to a new list
    new_list=old_list.copy()
    #loop to read all the tuples in the list
    for t in range(len(tuple_list)):
    #Assign tuple values to variables
        start_na=tuple_list[t][0] #starting index in the list
        end_na=tuple_list[t][1]   #ending index in the list
        previous_value=tuple_list[t][2] #previous real value
        next_value=tuple_list[t][3] #next real value
        for i in range (start_na,end_na+1):
            #In case where the start index of the Nan series is equal to 0
            if start_na==0:
                #replace all the elements with the next value
                new_list[i]=next_value
            #in case where the ending index of the Nan series is at the end of the list
            elif end_na==len(new_list)-1:
                #replace all elements with the previous value
                new_list[i]=previous_value
            #in the case where the Nan series has only one element
            elif start_na==end_na:
                #replace the element by the average of the previous and next values
                new_list[i]=(previous_value+next_value)/2

            #in cas where  all the previous conditions are not verified
            else:
            # replace the elements by the estimated progression between two real values
                new_list[i]=previous_value+(i-start_na+1)*((next_value-previous_value)/(end_na-start_na+2))
       
            
    return new_list

#Function to replace missing data by applying two previous functions

def fill_missing_df(df,start_row, end_row, start_col,end_col,percen_limit):
    #define a empty list to containe indexes of rows that will be droped
    drop_list=[]
    # loop on the df rows
    for r in range(start_row,end_row-start_row):
        #creation of a list containing all the original values of the row
        org_list=df.iloc[r,start_col:end_col].tolist()
        
        #count the number of NaN value in the org_list
        nan_count=sum(np.isnan(x) for x in org_list)
        limit=(end_col-start_col)*percen_limit/100

        #check if the count of NaN is greater than the accepted limit
        
        if nan_count > limit :
        #Add a the row index in a drop list
            drop_list.append(df.index[r])
           
        else:     
        #if the count of NaN is lower than the limit
            #get the list of missing data locations using missing_location function 
            location=missing_location(org_list)
            #get a new full list using fill_missing function 
            new_list=fill_missing_list(org_list,location)
            #replace the specific row with the new full list 
            for j in range(0,len(new_list)):
                df.iat[r,j+start_col]=new_list[j]
           
    #Drop rows if the pourcentage of Nan exceed the limit
    df.drop(drop_list,inplace=True)
    #reset the index of dataframe
    df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)
    #print out the droped rows index list and count
    print("{} rows are droped in your dataframe: {}".format(len(drop_list),drop_list))

def influence_plot(mydf,first_var,second_var):
                
        
        first_var_name=mydf.columns[first_var] #get the first variable name
        second_var_name=mydf.columns[second_var] # get the second variable name

        
        second_var_med=mydf.iloc[:,second_var].median() #Calculate the second variable median 
        low_query='{}<{}'.format(second_var_name,second_var_med) # define a query statement for low then median dataframe
        hight_query='{}>{}'.format(second_var_name,second_var_med)# define a query statement for hight then median dataframe

        low_df=mydf.query(low_query) # execute low_query and assign the result to low_df
        hight_df=mydf.query(hight_query) # execute hight_query and assign the result to hight_df


        
        location=[1,2] #list of bars locations 
        fig = plt.figure()  
        fig.set_size_inches(10,10) #set a bar plot size
        bar_width = 0.35 #set a bars width
        opacity = 0.4 #set a plot opacity
        heights = [low_df[first_var_name].mean(),hight_df[first_var_name].mean()] #Assign the bar plot values 
        labels = ['Low_{}'.format(second_var_name), 'High_{}'.format(second_var_name)] #assign the bars labels
        plt.bar( location, heights, tick_label=labels) # set a bar plot parameters 
        plt.title('{} by {} level'.format(first_var_name,second_var_name)) #set the plot title
        plt.xlabel('{} level'.format(second_var_name)) #set the plot x label
        plt.ylabel('{}'.format(first_var_name)) # set the plot y label
        plt.show()

def influence_plot_by_continent(mydf,first_var,second_var):
    #set a continent names in a list
    continent_list=mydf.continent.unique().tolist() #extract the continent names existing in the dataframe
    first_var_name=mydf.columns[first_var] #extract the first variable name
    second_var_name=mydf.columns[second_var] # extract the second variable name
    low_val=[] #this list will contain values  greater than the second variable median from each continent
    hight_val=[] #this list will contain values  less than the second variable median from each continent
    n_continent=len(continent_list) #number of continent in the list


    
    #loop to get the mean () of values less and more the median
    for i in range(len(continent_list)):
            current_continent=continent_list[i] #get the continent name

            df_continent=mydf.query('continent==@current_continent') # get a dataframe of selected continent     
            second_var_med=df_continent.iloc[:,second_var].median() # calculate the median for the specific variable
            low_query='{}<{}'.format(second_var_name,second_var_med) # define a query statement for low then median dataframe
            hight_query='{}>{}'.format(second_var_name,second_var_med) # define a query statement for hight then median dataframe

            low_df=df_continent.query(low_query) # execute low_query and assign the result to low_df
            hight_df=df_continent.query(hight_query) # execute hight_query and assign the result to hight_df

            low_val.append(float(low_df[first_var_name].mean())) #add the mean of the first variable column to low_val list
            hight_val.append(float(hight_df[first_var_name].mean()))#add the mean of the second variable column to hight list

    #plot the low_val with blue color 
    
    fig = plt.figure()  
    fig.set_size_inches(10,10) #set a bar plot size
    index = np.arange(0, n_continent * 2, 2) # bar location index
    bar_width = 0.35 #set a bars width
    opacity = 0.4 #set a plot opacity
    
    #plot the low_val side by side with blue color 
    low_bar = plt.bar(index, low_val, bar_width,
                 alpha=opacity,
                 color='b',
                 label='Low_{}'.format(second_var_name))
    
    #plot the hight_val side by side with green color 
    hight_bar= plt.bar(index + bar_width, hight_val, bar_width,
                 alpha=opacity,
                 color='g',
                 label='hight_{}'.format(second_var_name))
    


    plt.ylabel(first_var_name) #set the plot bar y label
    plt.title('{} by {} level'.format(first_var_name,second_var_name)) #set the plot bar title
    plt.xticks(index + bar_width, continent_list, rotation='vertical') #Set the continent names as x labels in the vertical rotation
    plt.legend(loc='best') # set the legend location in the best place
    plt.show()

Limitation¶

The limitation of this project are:

  • Missing data: the approach adopted to fill in the missing data can impact the final result especially when you want to analyze trends certain values are estimated.
  • Droped countries: I droped 23 countries which could impact the analysis result especially in the analysis by continent.
  • Population not included: for the analysis at the modial scale and even in the analysis by continent it is perhaps better to reason by the average weighted by the population, and not to consider that all the countries have the same population, China does'nt have the same weight as Montenegro there is a big difference between the population of this two countries.
  • Income, Human development index and democracy index is not homogeneous between continents: this difference will impact the analysis when looking at countries between high and low level for each continent, for example to study the influence of income on the hapiscore we will have a large part classified among the high income unlike in Africa where we will have the largest part is classified as low income level.
  • the variables can be interrelated: obviously citizens in democratic countries around the world generally have a high income and their governments make more efforts in terms of human development, otherwise a higher income allows a better quality of health care then a much better life expectancy

Conclusions¶

Contrary to the common maxim which says that money does not buy happiness, our analysis shows that the countries with the highest income are the most happiest. the most significant effect of income on hapiscore is observed in Europe. In hight income countries the democracy level has a positif impact on the hapiscore, the more countries tend to adopt a democratic regime, the happier their people are, this observation on a global scale does not seem applicable in africa as long as the reverse is observed the more countries adopt a non-democratic regime the happier their people are. In low income countries more the country is evolved in terms of human development the happier their people are, but in Europe this infulence of humain developemnt index is weak. the most happiest countries has on average:
